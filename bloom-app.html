B2C application "Bloom"

PROJECT OVERVIEW

Category:
Mobile app design, UX/UI design


Timeline: 4 weeks


# of Team members: solo


Skills:
Comparative analysis, User Interviews, Synthesis, Ideating, User flows, App map, Wireframing, Prototyping, and Usability testing.


Tools:
Sketch, InVision

Everyone loves plants. They improve air quality and humidity levels, absorb noise, reduce stress, and create a feeling of comfort. However, maintaining a plant requires regular care that we often fail to provide. Have you ever happened to forget to water your plant? Has any of your home favorites died from either lack of or excessive daylight?

"Bloom" - an application that helps users choose a new home plant, identify plants and get approachable care recommendations. Watering and fertilizing schedules and notifications make growing plants easier than ever.

"Who will benefit from this app? - Everyone!"

Outcomes

User perspective

  • Users will keep their plants alive and healthy.
  • Preset reminders will help to provide on-time care and eliminate the need to remember unnecessary information.

Business perspective

  • An additional way of advertising for local plant stores.
  • Significant increase in sales volume.
  • The app is a good source of loyal customers.

UX Research

Proto-persona

Working on this project, my research targeted the specific group of people who do not consider themselves being good at growing plants but still like decorating home with them.

    Frequent habits:
  1. Puts plants closer to the window.
  2. Places plants at the spot where it will look nice depending on the interior.
  3. Never uses fertilizer.
  4. Tries to water frequently, but often forgets to water on time.
  5. While buying a new plant makes a decision based on:
  • Appearance
  • Size
  • Fit in the room
  • Difficulty of care
  • Pet friendliness
    •

Novice in growing plants

    Motivations:
  1. Visual aesthetics.
  2. Make home cozy and welcoming.
  3. Pure air.
    Pain points:
  1. Often forgets to water her plants
  2. Due to the improper care plants are dying that makes her very upset.
  3. “I cannot handle growing anything but cacti”
User needs:
  1. Pick the right plant that can survive at the place it is going to live.
  2. Easy way to choose a plant.
  3. Care instructions presented in an approachable way.
  4. Reminders that fit your schedule.
  5. Get help with the plants they already have even if they don’t know the names.

Solution

Choose the right plant! Don’t know how to do it?

Start with the place! Just look around, and think at how many places in your room you would put a plant. The number you will get is often no more than 5, and, of course, some will work better than others for you.

Now when you look at the spot you picked, it is much easier to visualize what kind of plant you want: whether it is big or small, with or without flowers and so on so forth. Moreover, the spot you chose has certain daylight level that narrows down the list of plants you can put there.

Follow the schedule!

Even if you are a busy bee there is must be a time in the week when you can spend a few minutes taking care of your home plants. To make sure it is consistent and you don’t forget to actually do it, you can set up reminders that will pop up at a specific time and days you want.

Sketching and wireframing

The idea of this app is pretty fresh, so it is important to show a novice user how it works first. That’s why my very first sketch started with the short demo

Wearing unusual features in familiar designs is also a good way to introduce them to the user. So I used the conventional design of the camera while scanning the brightness level in your room. Moreover, the phone camera is technically involved in this feature.

The same idea stands for turning on reminders where notification setting works the same way as the regular iPhone settings.

Usability testing

The very first issue in the prototype I noticed was in the demo. Three dots that were supposed to show the progress through the demo and give a hint that screens need to slide from the right did not work out. Users were lost and tried to exit after the first instruction. As a result, I switched from dots to an arrow and added numeration to point the user his “location” at the demo.

The initial design for the page of the plant that was already added in “my plants” list included only reminders settings and no information about the plant at all. I have no idea how could I missed it! I am really thankful to the users who pointed it to me as I had a chance to add a description and care instructions that play an important role in the app.

Probably the biggest challenge I faced was the location and design of the “add to my plants” button. I made a few variations and had a new cycle of testing after each twig. First, I placed it as just an icon in the right upper corner and almost everyone could not find it during the test. Then I moved it to the center and put the text next to the icon. The results were much better, but still not good enough. Moreover, placing the button in the center of the screen makes it inconvenient to press so that was a bad idea. The decision to put this button along with the main “go shopping” button looked pretty obvious for me. However, my concern was whether or not it will look like these buttons are equal. To avoid this misunderstanding, I decided to make “add” button little smaller, showing that “go shopping” is still dominant and more important.

My takeaways

  • If conventions exist, follow them.
  • Wearing unusual features in familiar designs is also a good way introduce them to the user.
  • Do not make your user think.
