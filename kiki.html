E-commerce platform for a local grocery store (conceptual)

PROJECT OVERVIEW

Category:
UX Design, Information Architecture (IA)


Timeline: 2 weeks sprint


# of Team members: solo


My role:
Designing the mid-fidelity prototype with a clear IA structure based on the combination of user’s needs and the goals of the business and its brand.


Tools:
Axure, Optimal Workshop

Kiki’s Kwik Mart is a little grocery store in Brighton, MA. It specializes in Irish food and has a wide variety of international products. That store is amazing! In Kiki’s you can find really unique products you won’t be able to see in traditional supermarkets. It has been serving delicious food to the community for more than three decades, but there’s no way to look up the products online.

Results:

The e-commerce platform has been added to the local grocery store. Thus, MA residents have got an opportunity to have products they like delivered and the business is gaining a significant increase in sales.

Research

I started my research conducting a series of interviews. With the use of screener questions I targeted audience that were either customers in that particular store or just international food shoppers. My goal was to figure out the shopping habits they have and the problems they face. I asked a number of questions starting from “What you don’t like about shopping international products?” to “How often do you notice the products on sale?”.

Problem statement

For some people knowing the ingredients is life important.

The interviews gave me a pretty clear insight that shopping international food can be really problematic!

  • The stores that are specialized in international food are not as accessible as regular supermarkets. Even if you are a huge fan of Irish food but you live far from Brighton, you may not want to spend time to get there.

  • Most of the products are unfamiliar as they often labeled and described in its origin language. The main pain point is that when you buy the product, you don’t know what is it, how is it going to taste or if you can actually eat it.

Hypothesis

I believe that by adding e-commerce to the website that has an efficient navigation and product description will help international food lovers to buy the products they want more easily. I will know this to be true when I see that customers from any part of MA can find the products they need and have them delivered from Kiki’s Kwik Mart.

Card sorting

In order to build intuitive navigation, I made a list of 100 products from the store and conducted 5 open and 3 closed card sorts. The categorization of some products got really tricky. There were category names that were just too specific for the wide range of products that Kiki’s offer (e.g. the “Cheese” category or “insect repellent”).

So I united some categories which looked similar and created subcategories for those that stood out. After that I did closed card sorts and tried them out making iterations based on user’s feedback after each session.

User flows

Working on the user flows I focused on the idea “I am not my user”. People are different and the paths they take to complete the task also vary. Keeping that in mind, I created 6 possible ways that can be used to find the product. Once it is done and the user is ready to check out, it is important to make sure there is easy way to do so.

Usability testing

After a round of usability testing, I noticed the tendency that users do not pay attention to filters and not use them even if they are challenged to find something very specific. As a result, I highlighted the headings making them bold and put the headings closer to checkboxes based on the principle of proximity. That little twig helped me to make filters more visible and clearer for the user.

The checkout form was initially located on single page with the areas that fold up as the user fills out the fields. However, tests have shown that it is confusing, because that way user did not have a chance to confirm the action. The solution I found is to break down the form to three pages and use the “Save” buttons. I also used the breadcrumb to show how many steps are left. Such iteration helped users easily complete the payment and place the order.

Project takeaways:

  • Users need to see a response to the actions they make.
  • Users tend to create the categories they used to see before.
  • All the important information must stand out to catch user's eye.
  • People think differently.
